\vspace{0.1cm} \noindent {\large \bf Introduction} Giant Molecular Clouds (GMCs) are key players in a galaxy’s evolution, yet howthe atomic interstellar medium (ISM) converts into molecules to become stellarnurseries is still poorely understood.  The neutral ISM consists mostly ofatomic hydrogen (\hi) in two flavors: the cold neutral medium (CNM) envelopedby the warm neutral medium (WNM) (McKee \& Ostriker 1977).  It is deep insidethe CNM clouds that hydrogen and most other elements are likely to exist inmolecular form,  providing conducive conditions for star formation.  Recently,Krumholtz et al. (2009; hereafter K+09) devised an analytical model formolecular hydrogen (\htwo) formation in equilibrium in an atomic cloud bathedin a radiation field. The model has important predictions: \htwo\ will formwithin a CNM cloud after a threshold \hi\ surface density (\hisd) is achievedto shield \htwo\ from photodissociation. After this point, the \hi\ contentremains constant while the \htwo\ surface density (\htwosd) sharply increases.The transition between \hi\ and \htwo\ has been identified in galaxies on kpcscales as an overall saturation of the \hi\ surface density (e.g., Bigiel etal. 2008; Wong et al. 2009).  Lee et al. (2012) tested the K+09 modelobservationally for the first time on sub-pc scales by identifying individualcloud cores in the Perseus molecular cloud.  This study suggests that basicprinciples of \htwo\ formation in equilibrium with photodissociation aresound.  Perseus is a relatively queiscent cloud with solar metallicity and nomassive internal stars, thus a burning question remains: how does \htwo\ formin different environments?\textbf{ I will use a wealth of {\bf H\,{\sc i}}, carbon monoxide (CO), andfar-infrared observations of six Galactic molecular clouds (Taurus, California,Rosette, Mon OB1, NGC 2264, and Orion) in various stages of chemical andstellar evolution to test how the radiation field, star formation rate, gastemperature, and {\bf H\,{\sc i}} surface density affect the formation of {\bfH$_{\rm 2}$}.} I will build upon the analysis of Lee et al.  (2012), usingGalactic Arecibo L-band Feed Array (GALFA) \hi\ observations and dustextinction maps to determine the extent and the scales of the \hisd\ thresholdacross these clouds. I will measure the amount of \hi\ absorption using Areciboand Very Large Array observations and  derive \hi\ spin temperatures of theatomic envelopes around molecular cores.  If my work shows that the \hisd\ is areliable predictor of \htwo\ content across varying interstellar environments,future generation telescopes such as the Australian Square Kilometer ArrayPathfinder paired with optical extinction maps will be a valuable tool inprobing the \htwo\ abundance for large samples of galactic and extragalacticmolecular clouds. \\\vspace{-.3cm} \noindent {\large \bf Intellectual Merit} Several steady state, chemical equilibrium models have found the timescale of\htwo\ formation to be 10-30 Myr (Goldsmith et al.  2007; Liszt 2007).  This iscomparable to or even longer than GMC lifetimes and thus modeling has payedsignificant attention to interstellar turbulence, where a large clumping factorincreases the \htwo\ formation rate. Glover et al. (2010) found frommagnetohydrodynamic (MHD) simulations coupled with a simple chemical networkthat a significant amount of \htwo\ forms within 1-2 Myr.  In contrast to thesteady state, chemical equilibrium model, \htwo\ does not reach chemicalequilibrium in the MHD model. Gas will become fully molecular unless themolecular cloud is destroyed by stellar feedback. The conflicting formationscenarios of \htwo\ thus beg for further observational tests. The K+09equilibrium model connects 3D physical quantities (such as volume density) witheasily observable quantities (gas surface density), allowing for directobservational tests.\begin{comment}The details of the formation of \htwo\ in GMCs are still very much underdebate. Several authors have found that in equilibrium, where \htwo\ formationand photodissociation are balanced, the \htwo\ formation timescale is$\sim10$-30 Myr (Goldsmith et al.  2007; Liszt 2007). This is comparable to orlarger than GMC lifetimes constrained observationally, thus modeling has payedsignificant attention to interstellar turbulence as a possible agent forspeeding up H2 formation.  For example, Glover et al. (2010) found frommagnetohydrodynamical simulations that \htwo\ could form in only 1-2 Myr, withall atomic hydrogen in a GMC becoming molecular unless \htwo\ formation wasterminated by stellar feedback. The conflicting formation scenarios of \htwo\thus beg for further observational tests. The  K+09 equilibrium model connects3D physical quantities (such as volume density) with easily observablequantities (gas surface density), allowing for direct observational tests.\end{comment}My first steps toward testing K+09's model and the formation mechanism of\htwo\ will be to identify and isolate the \hi\ envelope around cloud cores. Iwill propagate a Gaussian spectral decomposition across the six GMCs and thenmatch \hi\ velocity components with the CO observations from the CfA survey(Dame et al. 2001), thereby confining the envelope extent in a reproduciblemanner.
